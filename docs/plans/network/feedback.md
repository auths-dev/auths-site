This is a well-constructed argument built around one correct and clarifying observation, and its main weakness is that the observation is doing more rhetorical work than technical work. Let me separate what holds up from what I'd push on.

**The core thesis is right, and worth stating plainly.** Preventing double-spend of a *bearer* asset forces a single total order and therefore consensus; verifying *authorization and provenance* over account-based settlement does not. That's a real distinction and the paper draws it cleanly. The reductions are honest — tamper-evidence to collision-resistance (Prop 2), authorization-unforgeability to EUF-CMA (Prop 3) — and the KERI analogy is the right anchor, since KERI already made exactly this "per-controller log, not global chain" move for identity. So far, sound.

**But the interesting security is being done by the rail and the anchors, not by the hash chains — and the paper knows this while framing around it.** The triumphant line ("Bitcoin's tamper-evidence without Bitcoin's blockchain") holds only because two hard things are delegated out:

- *Single-settlement* is handed to the external rail. Fine, but that means the property most people actually worry about (spending the same dollar twice, a runaway loop draining a budget across concurrent calls) is enforced elsewhere, and the paper's contribution is the orthogonal, easier provenance layer. That's a legitimate decomposition, but it makes §3's Proposition 1 close to trivial — it's "a conjunction of local predicates is local," dressed up. The load-bearing move isn't the proposition, it's the modeling decision to push settlement onto the rail.

- *Freshness/completeness* is the genuinely hard part, and §5.3 is where the "no consensus" claim quietly acquires its asterisk. Tamper-evidence protects a *presented* log; it does nothing against a party that withholds recent records or rolls back. The fix is the "anchoring ladder" — but every rung except trust-on-first-seen reintroduces exactly what the paper claims to escape: a witness (a trusted third party for *ordering*), a transparency co-signature, or literally an on-chain anchor. So the honest statement is narrower than the abstract: you get tamper-evidence with no consensus, but you get *completeness* only by reintroducing an ordering authority. Double-spend prevention was itself a freshness problem, which is why consensus earned its keep — and freshness is where this construction still has to pay, just in a different currency.

**Availability being a non-goal undercuts the accountability claim more than the paper admits.** For a dispute, the failure mode isn't a *forged* log, it's a *missing* one. A dishonest party that simply declines to present its chain defeats the whole apparatus, and cryptographic tamper-evidence buys you nothing there. The anchoring/witness layer is the real answer, but it's the least developed section and it's where the trust you removed comes back.

**The related-work section overstates novelty by omission.** The construction is essentially: certificate-transparency-style tamper-evident logs + KERI delegated identifiers + attenuable capability tokens. The closest industrial precedent — AWS QLDB — is almost precisely "per-party signed hash chain, no consensus, cryptographically verifiable," and it isn't mentioned. Neither are transparency logs generally (Trillian, Rekor/Sigstore) nor macaroons and biscuit tokens, which are the delegated, attenuable capability the "mandate" reinvents. Citing these wouldn't sink the paper — the *composition* aimed at agent payments is a reasonable contribution — but a reviewer will notice, and their absence lets the paper imply more originality than the "we add nothing to the cryptographic toolbox" admission actually supports.

**The game-theory section (§6) is the softest.** The receipt-lottery analogy is charming but loose. Nota Fiscal Paulista worked because the *state* ran the lottery, cross-checked filings, and the buyer had a concrete positive incentive to demand the receipt. Here, if publishing logs is optional and availability is a non-goal, "no party knows who will re-derive its log" loses its teeth — the deterrent needs the witness layer to be real and somewhat mandatory, which again pulls back toward the trusted party the paper wants to avoid. The mechanism isn't wrong, but it's borrowed enforcement that the construction doesn't itself provide.

A couple of smaller flags a careful reader will raise: the linear per-party chain serializes concurrent calls, so under a multi-instance gateway it's unclear who assigns `b_{i-1}` — a throughput and correctness wrinkle worth a sentence. And revocation checking (`a revocation ≤ b_n exists`) is itself a freshness problem inheriting all the KEL-availability caveats, so the redirect-attack corollary is only as strong as witness coverage of the principal's KEL.

On presentation: it's genuinely well-written, and the "honesty machines" through-line is effective. The one stylistic thing I'd cut is the "read left to right" gloss appended to every formula — it reads as padding after the second occurrence, and slightly signals a worry that the math won't stand on its own.

Net: the paper is most valuable as a *conceptual reframe* — "agent-payment accountability is a provenance problem, not an agreement problem" — and that reframe is correct and useful. It's weakest wherever it needs freshness (truncation, revocation, availability), because that's the sliver where consensus was actually doing work, and the paper's answer there is "add a trusted witness," which deserves to be foregrounded as a real cost rather than tucked into a non-goals paragraph. If I were reviewing, I'd ask for: honest positioning against QLDB / transparency logs / macaroons, a sharper treatment of the witness layer as load-bearing rather than optional, and a more rigorous (or more modest) §6.
